ECHO
RITUAL 💐

Spatial emotional experience system for unresolved grief after sudden loss

The funeral ends. Grief doesn't.
We designed a place where grief can continue to exist.

My Role

# User Research & Interviews # Insight Synthesis & Principles # Spatial Experience Concept # Blender Hi-fi Rendering # AI Video Prototyping

01 · Project Overview

Echo Ritual is a spatial experience design project focused on unresolved grief after sudden loss. Through user research, insight synthesis, and systematic spatial design, it builds an emotional experience system where grief can be accepted, expressed, and witnessed.

Background

Existing grief support centres on a single funeral ritual. Once it ends, emotional support abruptly disappears. The words left unsaid, unfinished farewells, and memories with nowhere to go lack any space within existing social structures.

Value

Translating the psychological concept of 'unresolved grief' into a perceivable, interactive spatial experience, exploring the design boundaries of emotional-support service experiences.

Tools & Timeline

Duration: 3 months

Research: Semi-structured interviews · Journey Map · Affinity Map

Design: Figma · Blender · AI Video

Collaboration: Group project · individually led research & spatial design

02 · Problem Discovery

Why This Problem

Grief support systems almost entirely focus on the 72 hours around the funeral. Yet psychological research shows that unresolved grief continues for months or even years.

Core Question

After the ceremony ends, where do the words left unsaid go? Can we design a place for grief that continues to exist?

User Pain Points

  • Broken expression: When the funeral ends, it signals 'time to move on' — yet there is nowhere left for emotions to go.
  • Homeless memories: Objects, sounds, and memories connected to the deceased lack a carrier.
  • Social isolation: Those around gradually withdraw, unsure how to respond, deepening loneliness.
  • Missing ritual: With no place to return to, grief is processed alone in private.

Design Opportunity

Design an emotional space people can return to repeatedly — not a one-time ritual. Let grief unfold at its own rhythm rather than being forced toward closure.

03 · User Research

Research Methods

  • Semi-structured interviews: In-depth interviews with 6 participants who experienced sudden bereavement.
  • Desk research: Grief psychology, anthropology of mourning rituals, existing memorial space case studies.
  • Competitive analysis: Experience gaps in digital memorial platforms and physical memorial spaces.

Key Interview Quotes

"Everyone went back to work after the funeral, but I had no idea what I was supposed to do."

"There were so many things I never said. I don't know where to put them."

"I want to go back to that place, but that place doesn't exist."

Journey Map Key Findings

Mapping the emotional journey before and after the funeral identified three critical fracture points:

  • T+24h: Social support rapidly withdraws; the person faces their emotional peak alone.
  • T+2w: External pressure to 'return to normal' forces grief underground.
  • Long-term: Anniversaries, holidays, and other triggers repeatedly reactivate unresolved emotions.

Affinity Map Clusters

Interview content was grouped into four emotional themes: unsaid words / homeless memories / isolated grief / longing for ritual. These four themes directly map to the four design modules.

04 · Insights & Design Principles

Key Insights

  • Insight 01: Grief needs to be witnessed, not solved — people need to feel their grief is accepted, not to move past it.
  • Insight 02: Ritual activates emotional expression — rhythmic, repeatable ritual behaviour helps emotions release in an ordered way.
  • Insight 03: Collectivity makes loneliness bearable — knowing 'others feel the same' is more powerful than direct emotional support.
  • Insight 04: The unfinished conversation is the most lasting pain — giving people the chance to 'speak' to the deceased can repair emotional fractures.

Design Principles

  • Slow pace first: Spatial design does not pursue efficiency — it allows grief to unfold at its own speed.
  • Private and shared coexist: Protect personal emotion while offering optional moments of collective connection.
  • Return repeatedly: The experience is not one-time; the space allows continuous visits and accumulation.
  • Atmosphere as interaction: Light, sound, and floating sensations are themselves the design language.

Design Translation Logic

4 Insights → 4 Design Principles → 4 spatial modules. A clear derivation chain links research to design.

05 · Design Exploration

Early Concepts → Discarded

Concept A (Digital Memorial App): Initially considered a mobile app for uploading memories. Testing showed that the everyday feel of a phone screen conflicted with mourning emotions.

Concept B (Single Memorial Device): Designed a physical device for leaving voice messages. Interviews revealed users needed a place to keep returning to.

Key Pivot → Spatial Narrative

After two rounds of rejection, we realised: grief is fundamentally a journey, not a moment. The design medium had to shift from 'product' to 'space'.

We settled on a floating spatial system as the framework — the act of moving through space itself becomes the process of grief unfolding.

Iteration Logic

① Digital product → ② Physical device → ③ Spatial experience system. Each rejection was based on user testing feedback.

06 · Final Design

Echo Ritual is an emotional experience system comprising four spatial modules. Users move along a floating pathway, progressing from private reflection toward collective connection.

Module 01 · Lily Pad Pathway

User action: Step slowly across floating lily pads; your pace sets the movement rhythm.

System response: Each step triggers ripples and low-frequency ambient sounds; the path shifts slightly with mood.

Design goal: Use movement itself to establish emotional ritual, entering a state of mourning at a slow pace.

Linked insight: Insight 02 — Ritual activates emotional expression.

Module 02 · Memory Tree

User action: Speak to the trunk, upload a photo or recording — content attaches to branches as points of light.

System response: The tree grows slowly as content accumulates; the light points gently breathe at night.

Design goal: Provide a concrete physical vessel for 'words that were never said'.

Linked insight: Insight 04 — The unfinished conversation is the most lasting pain.

Module 03 · Poem Making Island

User action: Select from floating word fragments, assembling a poem that belongs only to you.

System response: Once complete, the poem drifts into the water as a paper of light, encountering others' poems along the current.

Design goal: Transform hard-to-articulate emotions into creative action, lowering the barrier to expression.

Linked insight: Insight 01 — Grief needs to be witnessed, not solved.

Module 04 · Memory Wall

User action: Browse memory fragments left by others; light up one to say 'me too'.

System response: Lit memories briefly glow and emit a soft resonant sound.

Design goal: Create collective emotional connection without exposing privacy.

Linked insight: Insight 03 — Collectivity makes loneliness bearable.

07 · Video Prototype

Why Video, Not Figma

The core experience depends on atmosphere, rhythm, and spatial depth — qualities impossible to convey through a Figma prototype.

The video prototype allows reviewers to fully experience the emotional journey in 90 seconds.

Production Process

  • Blender: Built high-fidelity 3D scenes for each of the four spatial modules.
  • AI video generation: Assisted in generating part of the environmental motion effects in the dynamic prototype.
  • Narrative structure: The video is ordered by the user's emotional journey, not by function demonstration order.

AI's Role in the Design Process

AI tools handled 'efficiency layer' tasks. Design decisions were still driven by research insights. AI accelerated expression but did not replace design judgement.

08 · Reflection

Biggest Challenge

Finding the balance between 'design aesthetics' and 'genuine emotional weight'. Early concepts were too visually polished — users said they didn't feel like 'a real place for grief'.

Next Iterations

  • Collaborate with grief counsellors to validate the actual effectiveness of the spatial experience.
  • Develop an accessible digital twin version so remote users can still participate.
  • Introduce a time dimension — let the space change as visits and time accumulate.

Key Takeaways

  • Spatial rhythm and atmosphere can become an independent interaction language, independent of screens and interfaces.
  • The value of research-driven design: every spatial module can be traced back to a specific user insight.
  • AI tools significantly improved design communication efficiency during the prototyping stage.

HELENSBURGH
GLOW WORM
MICROSITE

Responsive ecological tourism microsite for safer, low-impact night exploration

Visitors often arrive without guidance. We designed the preparation they never had.

My Role

# UX Research # Information Architecture # Responsive Web Design # Front-end Development

01 · Project Overview

The Helensburgh Glow Worm Tunnel is a fragile ecological habitat and a popular night-visit destination in NSW. This project designed a responsive microsite to help visitors plan safe, informed, and low-impact visits.

Background

Located near Helensburgh, NSW, the tunnel was originally historic railway infrastructure, later transformed into a glow worm habitat. It attracts night explorers — yet there is no official digital presence to guide visitor behaviour.

Project Value

Demonstrate how responsible UX design can protect fragile natural environments by making ecological rules and planning information more accessible, reducing harmful visitor behaviour before it happens.

Tools & Timeline

Duration: 3 weeks

Research: Competitive analysis · User scenario mapping

Design: Figma · HTML · CSS

Collaboration: Individual project

02 · Problem Discovery

Why This Problem

Glow worms are extremely sensitive to light and noise. Yet most visitors arrive without knowing this. Existing travel blogs, forums, and council pages are fragmented, outdated, or hard to use on mobile in the field.

Core Question

How might we help visitors explore the tunnel safely and confidently while protecting a fragile glow worm colony?

User Pain Points

  • No trip planning support: Transport options, opening status, and safety conditions are scattered across multiple sources.
  • No behavioural guidance: Visitors don't know what lighting, noise level, or conduct is appropriate around glow worms.
  • Poor mobile experience: All existing information sources are not optimised for mobile use at the site.
  • Low confidence: Without clear guidance, visitors are unsure whether their visit will be worthwhile or safe.

03 · User Research

Research Methods

  • Competitive analysis: Audited 6 nature tourism microsites and national park information platforms for structural and content benchmarks.
  • User scenario mapping: Mapped three visitor archetypes to identify distinct information needs at each journey stage.
  • Desk research: Reviewed ecological guidance for bioluminescent habitats and low-impact tourism standards.

Key Research Findings

  • Visitors make go/no-go decisions in under 3 minutes of online research — the site must surface critical information immediately.
  • 'Is it worth going tonight?' is the most common question — real-time status and conditions are the highest priority.
  • Visitors are willing to follow ecological rules if they are presented clearly and non-judgementally.

Information Architecture Decision

Structured the site around four user intents: Explore / Visit / Guidelines / Gallery. Each section maps directly to a user need identified in research.

04 · Insights & Design Principles

Key Insights

  • Insight 01: Information asymmetry causes harm — the main reason visitors damage the habitat is not malice, but ignorance.
  • Insight 02: Atmosphere is not decoration — the visual identity should convey the fragility and beauty of the environment, making users emotionally invested in protecting it.
  • Insight 03: Mobile-first is non-negotiable — visitors are using their phones at the trailhead, not a desktop at home.
  • Insight 04: Real-time confidence beats long-form content — a single status widget ('Open tonight · Low crowds') is worth more than three paragraphs of description.

Design Principles

  • Information before arrival: All critical information must be accessible before the visitor leaves home.
  • Guidance without moralising: Ecological rules are presented as helpful tips, not prohibitions.
  • Environment-first visual language: Dark UI, glow particles, and tunnel textures reflect the actual experience of the site.
  • Responsive as a baseline: Every layout decision was tested at 375px mobile width first.

Design Translation Logic

4 Insights → 4 Principles → 4 site sections. Research directly drove both the content architecture and the visual direction.

05 · Design Exploration

Early Concepts → Discarded

Concept A (Light-themed UI): Initial wireframes used a bright aesthetic. User feedback: it felt like a generic hiking blog — it didn't communicate the unique darkness and intimacy of the glow worm experience.

Concept B (Single-page scroll): A long single-page format buried critical planning information — users abandoned before reaching transport details.

Key Pivot → Dark Immersive Design

Switched to a dark UI with animated glow effects and a bioluminescent colour palette. This immediately differentiated the site and created an emotional connection to the actual tunnel experience.

Multi-page architecture with a persistent hover dropdown navigation replaced the single-page layout, letting users jump directly to their most relevant section.

Iteration Logic

① Generic tourism → ② Single-page dump → ③ Dark immersive multi-section. Each pivot driven by user testing feedback.

06 · Final Design

A dark, immersive responsive microsite that gives visitors everything they need before, during, and after their visit — while embedding ecological responsibility into every interaction.

Key Design Decisions

  • Move 01 — Hover Dropdown Navigation: Lets users access any sub-section from any page without cognitive overload.
  • Move 02 — Planning Widget Cluster: Search bar, embedded map, weather widget, and site status indicator grouped together to answer 'Can I go tonight?' in one glance.
  • Move 03 — Eco-Behaviour Guidelines: Do/Don't format with positive framing — 'Bring a red-light torch' rather than 'Don't use white light'.
  • Move 04 — Atmosphere as Interaction: Animated firefly/glow particles on scroll create immersion and reinforce the site's tunnel identity.

Technical Implementation

  • Fully responsive layout — tested at 375px, 768px, and 1280px breakpoints
  • CSS animation for glow particle effects — no JavaScript dependency
  • Semantic HTML structure for accessibility
  • Embedded Google Maps integration for transport planning

Design ↔ Code

Every visual decision was validated by writing the actual CSS — if it couldn't be built cleanly, the design was revised.

07 · Reflection

Biggest Challenge

Balancing information density with visual atmosphere. The solution was a strict hierarchy: atmosphere first, critical information second, supporting detail third — each with a distinct visual weight.

Next Iterations

  • Integrate live API data for real-time weather and site status updates
  • Conduct usability testing with first-time visitors on-site to validate navigation decisions
  • Develop an offline mode so visitors can access guidelines without mobile signal in the tunnel

Key Takeaways

  • Information architecture is the backbone of UX — getting the structure right made every visual and interaction decision easier.
  • Design has a responsibility beyond aesthetics: in this case, visual design choices directly influenced ecological behaviour.
  • Designing and building simultaneously forces honest design decisions — if you can't code it cleanly, reconsider the design.

AI MUSIC
LEARNING
SYSTEM ♪

AI-assisted music learning app centred on adaptive feedback and emotional engagement

Practice is not just about accuracy.
Motivation and emotional connection matter just as much.

My Role

# UX Research & Interviews # Interaction Design # Interface Prototyping # User Testing

01 · Project Overview

AI Music Learning System reimagines AI's role in music education — shifting it from a correction tool to a collaborative emotional partner. Through user research and Figma prototyping, the project builds a learning experience that adapts to both technical performance and emotional state.

Background

With the rapid growth of AI in education, music apps use AI for technical feedback but consistently ignore motivation, emotional engagement, and the human joy of making music.

Project Value

Explore what it means to design AI as an emotionally intelligent partner rather than an assessment tool, and how adaptive pacing and encouraging feedback can keep learners engaged over time.

Tools & Timeline

Duration: 2 months

Research: Semi-structured interviews · Competitive analysis

Design: Figma · AI ideation tools

Collaboration: Individual project

02 · Problem Discovery

Why This Problem

The dropout rate for self-taught music learners is extremely high. The primary reason is not difficulty — it's a lack of motivation and emotional connection to practice. Existing AI tools double down on correctness, which increases anxiety for beginners.

Core Question

How might AI support music learners not just technically, but emotionally — keeping them motivated through the long, repetitive process of building skill?

User Pain Points

  • Correction-only feedback: Apps constantly highlight mistakes without acknowledging progress, making practice feel punishing rather than rewarding.
  • No adaptive pacing: Practice difficulty doesn't adjust to the learner's current energy, mood, or progress — leading to frustration or boredom.
  • Impersonal experience: Generic feedback that doesn't feel like it was written for this learner's specific journey.
  • No celebration of effort: The emotional dimension of learning — pride in finally playing a difficult passage correctly — is never acknowledged.

03 · User Research

Research Methods

  • Semi-structured interviews: Interviewed 5 music learners at different skill levels about their practice experience, emotional relationship with music, and experiences with AI tools.
  • Competitive analysis: Analysed Simply Piano, Yousician, and Flowkey to map feature gaps, emotional tone, and feedback mechanisms.

Key Interview Quotes

"AI is creative and informative but lacks emotional depth." — Rose

"AI is not very good at human emotions." — Milikiauk

"When I finally get a piece right, I want something to celebrate with me — not just tell me the next mistake."

Competitive Analysis Findings

  • All existing apps prioritise technical correctness metrics over emotional engagement indicators.
  • None offer adaptive difficulty based on detected emotional state (frustration, fatigue, excitement).
  • Reward systems are limited to streaks and badges — no acknowledgement of the quality or feeling of a session.

User Journey Map Insight

Mapping the practice session journey revealed a consistent emotional pattern: early optimism → mid-session frustration → potential abandon point → pride if pushed through. The design needed to support that transition.

04 · Insights & Design Principles

Key Insights

  • Insight 01: Encouragement outperforms correction — users who received encouraging feedback practiced longer and returned more often than those who received correction-only feedback.
  • Insight 02: Adaptive pacing is the most requested missing feature — learners want the app to know when they're tired and adjust, not just track errors.
  • Insight 03: Personalisation builds emotional investment — when the app 'knows' a learner's repertoire and goals, practice feels meaningful, not mechanical.
  • Insight 04: The moment of success needs to be witnessed — completing a difficult piece deserves more than a progress bar update.

Design Principles

  • Emotional feedback first: Every AI response acknowledges effort before addressing errors.
  • Adaptive by default: The system tracks not just correctness, but session length, error patterns, and speed to infer emotional state and adjust difficulty.
  • Celebrate progress, not just perfection: The reward system responds to improvement over time, not just flawless execution.
  • Personal space design: The home screen is a personalised 'practice space' the learner decorates and owns, increasing emotional investment.

Design Translation Logic

4 Insights → 4 Principles → 4 core interface features. The research chain is traceable at every design decision.

05 · Design Exploration

Early Concepts → Discarded

Concept A (AI Tutor Avatar): An animated AI character that reacted to performance. User testing found the humanoid avatar created unrealistic expectations — users felt more anxious when 'disappointing' it.

Concept B (Score-only feedback): A dashboard of performance metrics. Users found it demotivating: 'It feels like a test, not practice.'

Key Pivot → Companion System

Moved away from avatar and metrics toward an ambient intelligence model — the AI responds through environmental changes rather than explicit statements. This created emotional resonance without creating anxiety.

The home screen evolved from a generic dashboard to a personal practice room the learner customises — creating ownership and increasing return visits.

Iteration Logic

① Assessment tool → ② Animated tutor → ③ Ambient companion. Each pivot addressed a specific emotional friction point from user testing.

06 · Final Design

An AI-supported music learning app built around four core interaction modules — each designed to address a specific emotional need identified in research. The system adapts to both technical performance and emotional state in real time.

Module 01 · Library

User action: Browse instruments and music categories through a structured filtering system; save pieces to a personal repertoire.

Design goal: Let learners own their musical identity — the pieces they choose reflect who they are, not who the app tells them to be.

Module 02 · Practice Screen

User action: Practice with sheet music, playback controls, and real-time AI feedback in a single unified view.

System response: AI provides emotional tone-aware feedback — 'That section is coming together' before addressing specific errors.

Linked insight: Insight 01 — Encouragement outperforms correction.

Module 03 · Adaptive Practice Path

System behaviour: Tracks session length, error frequency, and interaction speed. If frustration signals are detected, the AI suggests a shorter warm-up or lowers difficulty.

Linked insight: Insight 02 — Adaptive pacing is the most requested missing feature.

Module 04 · Personal Practice Room

User action: Customise the home screen with furniture, plants, and decorative items unlocked through practice milestones.

Design goal: Create a space the learner feels belongs to them — increasing emotional investment in returning to practice.

Linked insight: Insight 03 — Personalisation builds emotional investment.

07 · Reflection

Biggest Challenge

Designing AI feedback that felt genuinely encouraging rather than condescending. Early prototypes were described as 'too cheerful'. The solution: calibrate tone to skill level — warmer for beginners, more concise and peer-level for advanced users.

Next Iterations

  • Develop and test the adaptive difficulty algorithm with real music learners over 4 weeks to validate its emotional impact
  • Explore multimodal feedback — microphone input for pitch detection and camera input for posture feedback
  • Design a social layer where learners can share their practice room and repertoire with friends

Key Takeaways

  • Emotional design is not decoration — it is the core functional requirement for any product that depends on sustained user motivation.
  • AI's role in a product is a design decision, not a technical given — choosing between corrector, coach, and companion has profound UX implications.
  • User testing at the concept stage (not just prototype stage) saved significant design time by revealing fundamental direction issues early.
